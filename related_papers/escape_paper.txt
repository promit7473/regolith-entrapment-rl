Skid-steered mobile robots (SSMRs) offer the advantages of a simple structure and good terrain adaptability,
making them widely used in agricultural production. The ground of outdoor orchards is generally undulating and
unpaved. When an SSMR operates on the undulating road, some tires lift off the ground and cannot provide
driving force, thus bringing the SSMR into a trapped state due to insufficient driving force. Moreover, the un­
dulating state of the outdoor orchard ground cannot be measured in real time due to the influence of vegetation
coverage. Therefore, the SSMR cannot avoid the undulating areas that may cause some tires to lift off the ground
through path planning methods. In this paper, the principles for the SSMR to be trapped due to partial tire lifting
off the ground were investigated, and a trapped-state recognition method based on internal sensor data of the
robots was established. On this basis, an escape action generation model of SSMR is designed, and the model
training was completed based on deep reinforcement learning. Finally, the simulation experiment and physical
experiment of SSMR escape are conducted. The experimental results showed that the methods proposed in this
paper could effectively recognize the trapped state of SSMR and achieved a 90 % success rate of escape.

1. Introduction
Skid-steered mobile robots (SSMRs) are widely used in various
agricultural production scenarios such as outdoor orchards due to their
advantages of compact and straightforward overall structure, strong
load-bearing capacity, and low manufacturing cost. During operation,
agricultural robots must walk along designated paths and perform pre­
determined operation tasks. Hence, path point tracking is a key function
for agricultural robots to complete operation tasks. For the path point
tracking problem of SSMR on undulating and unpaved roads, some
scholars have proposed many methods, such as model-based predictive
control (Prado, Á. J., et al., 2020; J. Prado et al., 2021), robust control
and adaptive control (J. Liao et al.,2019), and intelligent control
(Juman, M. A., et al., 2019).
However, on undulating unpaved roads, there may be soil slopes or
concavities, which can easily cause some of the tires of the SSMR to lift
off the ground and fail to provide driving force. Under the condition of
insufficient driving force, the SSMR will be trapped and unable to track
the target path (W. Yu et al., 2010). For the above-mentioned trappedstate problem, two solutions are usually adopted. The first step is to
install an auxiliary mechanism to prevent the robot from getting into

trouble (García, J. M., et al., 2017). However, the auxiliary mechanism
is costly, and it is only suitable for equipment with a lower weight. The
other solution is to enable the robot to avoid the zones that are prone to
trapping the SSMR by rational path planning. For example, the zones
that are prone to trapping the robot can be identified based on ground
elevation information (M. Toscano-Moreno et al., 2024) or ground
three-dimensional real-time perception information (R. Grandia
et al.,2023). Nevertheless, in the outdoor orchard environment, on the
one hand, the operation process of agricultural machinery will change
the undulating state of the road surface, making it difficult to obtain
stable elevation information; on the other hand, during the leaf-full and
leaf-dead periods, the vegetation covers the ground of outdoor orchards,
making it impossible to observe the terrain in real time, as illustrated in
Fig. 1.
Hence, in the outdoor orchard environment, it is challenging to avoid
an SSMR from falling into the trapped state by rational path planning.
Therefore, studying the autonomous escape methods of SSMR under the
condition of lacking real-time terrain information can improve the
reliability and effectiveness of SSMR operation in the outdoor orchard
environment, and has definite practical value.

There are relatively few investigations on the escape methods of SSMR

well as the path point tracking methods after SSMR escape. Based on the
reasons described above, this study investigates the problems of
autonomous escape and path point tracking of the SSMR based on in­
ternal sensor feedback and DRL. A method for identifying the trapped
state of the SSMR is designed based on the real-time torque information
feedback from the drive motor. Furthermore, a generation model for the
autonomous escape actions of the SSMR is designed and trained based
on LSTM and DRL. The proposed autonomous escape method for robots
based on internal feedback can effectively improve the reliability and
stability of SSMRs operating under unpaved, undulating road condi­
tions. This approach is demonstrated by taking an orchard with vege­
tation coverage and undulating terrain as the research environment.

2.3. Cause analysis of SSMR anomalies
When an SSMR operates on a locally undulating terrain, its abnormal
status is primarily caused by the failure of the grounded tires to provide
the driving force necessary for the SSMR to achieve its target running
status. During operations in outdoor orchards, an SMMR has relatively
low longitudinal velocity and yaw angular velocity. Therefore, the
anomaly of insufficient driving force is most commonly observed in skid
steering. If the yaw moment provided by the driving force of tires cannot
overcome the yaw resistance moment, the SSMR will fail to reach the
target motion state, yielding an anomaly. Therefore, this study analyzes
the causes of SSMR anomalies based on the yaw moment balance
equation. When the SSMR operates steadily with all four tires in normal

the torque output, the actuator has saturation characteristics. When an
SSMR steers in the case of the partial lift-off of tires, the torque required
to maintain the motion attitude of the SSMR exceeds the maximum
output capacity of the motor. Thus, the output torque of a grounded
motor will be close to its upper limit of torque output Tmax . The motor
will not be able to output the target torque required to maintain the
motion attitude, which leads to the SSMR’s anomaly. Therefore, when
the torque of a drive motor satisfies Eq., it is considered that there is an
SSMR anomaly.

4.4.2. Ablation experiments
Ablation experiments are conducted on the three modules to verify
the effects of LSTM, GAIL imitation learning, and manual reward. The
involved methods include the LGH method, the LH method (a method
including LSTM and Handcrafted Reward), the GH method (a method

and 21 depict the reward convergence curves and the success rate
convergence curves of different methods, respectively.
The experimental results show that in terms of the GAIL module, the
convergence speed of the methods containing the GAIL module (LGH,
GH, and LG) is faster than that of the methods without the GAIL module.
However, since the GH method does not have the time-series modeling
capability of the LSTM module, the decisions can only be made based on
the transient state of SSMR. As a result, although the GH method con­
verges faster than the LH method in the early stage, the convergence
process is unstable due to the lack of the LSTM module. Moreover, the
final effect is not better than that of the LH method.

method and the LG method in the early stage of training are comparable,
the LG method is characterized by a lower success rate of escape than the

1) Domain randomization
The gap between the simulation setup and reality can be mainly
attributable to drive motor parameters, sensor noises, and ambient
noises. This study performed extensive domain randomization on the
key dynamic parameters and ambient parameters of the robot to narrow
this gap. As shown in Table 9, ambient parameters, generated ground
height, ground complexity, the positions of the complex terrain and the
target point, the mass, center of gravity, and tire friction parameters of
the robot, and changes in its dynamic parameters were randomized to
allow the robot to reach different ground and target point positions. The
dynamic parameters of drive motors, including the maximum torque
and control parameters, were also randomized so that the policy could
adapt to changes in motor parameters, as well as parameter differences
caused by the assembly and running wear of the reducer. In addition,
due to installation errors, tire wear, and other reasons, the four tires of a
real SSMR were not always on the same plane. Consequently, the height
of the tire center was randomized in the simulated environment so that
the policy could adapt to the anomaly caused by the scenario of the four
tires not being on the same plane.
10,000 path spectra were designed based on the domain randomi­

in the simulation environment. One thousand path spectra that did not
participate in the training of the escape action generation model were
designed based on the domain randomization parameters in Table 9 to
test the effectiveness of the escape action generation model. The success
rate of escape control was obtained from the statistics of escape control
results on these 1,000 path spectra. Ultimately, the escape success rate
was 90 %.

5. Discussion
The results indicated that using the LGH method (i.e., the reward
fusion-based DRL policy) can complete the path-tracking task under
simulated and real-world outdoor undulating terrain conditions without
causing the SSMR to enter the abnormal status. The proposed reward
fusion-based method showed a specific generalization ability because of
the expert policy. There were only two basic sub-policies for avoiding
the SSMR anomalies in the expert policy: backward adjustment of the
heading angle and large-radius steering. However, when tracking the
second and fifth path points in Fig. 19, the final policy evaluated that the
first controlling angle could not track these path points. Accordingly, the
policy of driving forward to find a suitable position and then steering
was selected. Similarly, as shown in Fig. 23, in a real-world outdoor
scenario, the same policy was adopted when tracking the fifth path
point. As a result, it can be concluded that adopting the proposed reward
fusion-based DRL policy helped learn the expert policy and further
optimized the imitator policy based on the expert policy.

6. Conclusions

References

This paper proposed the trapped-state recognition method and
escape action generation method of an SSMR based on the internal
feedback information of the robot for the path point tracking control of
robots on unpaved outdoor roads, where the actual terrain undulations
could not be observed. This paper presented a method for recognizing
the trapped state based on the principal analysis results of the SSMR
being trapped on undulating road surfaces, established a simulation
environment for SSMR operation based on the Unity platform, and
completed the training of the SSMR escape action generation model
based on the DRL paradigm. The physical experiment of the robot was
completed in the outdoor orchard. It was found that the trapped state of
SSMR can be successfully detected and that the robot could autono­
mously escape from the trapped state. After escape, the robot can
autonomously track the subsequent path.
Four-wheel independent drive and independent steering (4WID4WIS) robots are also a commonly used type of mobile robot platform in
agricultural production operations. During the steering process of a
4WID-4WIS robot, the tire steering resistance increases due to terrain
conditions, preventing it from turning to the target angle. As a result, the
4WID-4WIS robot is trapped due to terrain factors. The method designed
in this paper started from the internal data feedback of the robot, and
could generate feasible escape actions when the robot was trapped.
Moreover, the method could establish the simulation object of the 4WID4WIS robot based on the existing simulation system design methods, and
simulate the terrain that was prone to trapping this type of robot in the
simulation environment. Furthermore, the method could design the
training parameters and reward functions required for DRL, and estab­
lish the escape action generation model required by the 4WID-4WIS
robot.
The research work of this paper is based on the premise that the
ground of outdoor orchards is relatively solid. However, the production
operations, such as ploughing and irrigation, can seriously change the
softness of the soil. The soil softness will change the output capacity of
the tire driving force, and the bulldozing effect will be more obvious.
Therefore, simulation environments must be established for different
ground conditions. Moreover, the effectiveness of the SSMR escape ac­
tion generation model and the adjustment of the model structure pro­
posed in this paper when ground conditions change have to be further
explored, improving the adaptability of our proposed method to the
changes of ground conditions.
